Okay, so when the government comes knocking on an AI lab's door, what happens? In the case of OpenAI and GPT-5.6, the answer is: a slower rollout. According to TechCrunch AI, OpenAI has throttled the release of its newest model after a government request, but the company isn't happy about it.

That's the interesting part. OpenAI didn't just comply quietly — they came out and basically said, "Hey, this isn't how we want to do business." In their own words: "We don't believe this kind of government access process should become the long-term default." And the reason is pretty straightforward. They argue it "keeps the best tools from users, developers, enterprises, cyber defenders, and global partners who need them." That's a long list of people being told to wait.

I get the tension here. On one hand, regulators probably have legitimate reasons to want eyes on frontier models before they hit the mainstream. On the other hand, OpenAI's point lands too — every day a capable model sits in limited release is a day that startups, security teams, and independent developers can't get their hands on tools that could genuinely help them. And once you set the precedent that governments can request delays, where exactly does it stop?

The real question is whether this is a one-off or the start of a pattern. If more labs start running into the same kind of friction, expect the AI policy fights in Washington to get a lot louder — and expect a lot more companies to start saying out loud what OpenAI just did.
