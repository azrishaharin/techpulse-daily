Okay so OpenAI finally did something about the prompt injection problem — sort of. The company just rolled out a new Lockdown Mode for ChatGPT, and the pitch is pretty straightforward: it should make it harder for sneaky prompt injections to trick the model into spilling your sensitive data.

But here's the part that made me do a double take. Even with Lockdown Mode switched on, ChatGPT could still be vulnerable to prompt injections. That's not me being cynical — that's basically the company admitting this is damage reduction, not a cure. The whole point of the feature is to reduce the likelihood that your private info gets leaked during a conversation, not to make those attacks impossible.

And honestly? That's a more honest framing than I expected. Prompt injection is one of those problems that keeps security researchers up at night because there's no clean fix. You can layer defenses, you can sandbox things, you can train the model harder — but the moment an LLM is parsing untrusted text, there's always some clever way to slip in instructions. OpenAI seems to know this, and instead of pretending they've solved it, they're shipping a mode that's designed to make the bad outcomes less likely.

What I want to watch now is how this plays out in the real world. Enterprises dealing with regulated data — healthcare, finance, legal — are exactly the folks who have been begging for something like this. If Lockdown Mode actually cuts down on the data exfiltration cases, it's a real win. If attackers can route around it as easily as the company hints they might, well, at least OpenAI didn't oversell the thing.
